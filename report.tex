\begin{abstract}
This report details the development of an evaluation framework designed to assess the capability of Large Language Models (LLMs)—specifically Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct—to recognise and analyse literature across multiple languages. Using text snippets automatically extracted and processed from Project Gutenberg, we tested the model's capacity to identify the correct book title from a set of four candidates. The evaluation spanned five languages: English, German, French, Italian, and Dutch. 

Beyond standard recognition, the framework incorporated several tests: cross-lingual evaluation (matching German translations to English titles to test translation application), robustness testing via noisy text, and an analysis of the model's reasoning for its choices. All results were visualised graphically. Across all variants, English consistently performed better than the other languages, indicating that the model's literary recognition capabilities are highly language-dependent, leading to weaker performance for users operating in non-English contexts.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
The rise of Artificial Intelligence in the last years, saw especially a big development in LLMs, Large Language models. Models who were trained on large chunks of text, and capable of reproducing text, based on this trainings data. Large language models answer questions in many languages, yet most of their training data is in English. The training data mainly stems from the internet, making it in large parts english and european centric. It is therefore reasonable to ask whether they perform as well in other languages as they do in English. The question
matters regarding trust and fairness. A model should be usable for all types of users, including those with different linguistic backgrounds. When a user in Germany addresses the model in German, they would not be able to notice whether the output has a better or worse quality than in other languages. 

The suggested course project approach was to take an existing benchmark, translate it with a chatbot and compare the scores before and after. During the project, we departed from this plan. While we stayed within the realm of assessing literature knowledge of LLMs, we shifted the focus.  
While machine translations often count as reliable, at least when done on well-trained languages, this mostly extends to a translation in a more technical way; the translation will probably carry the correct meaning, but it will lack the same style and artistic approach. Human book translations are well known for translating more the style of the book than the single words. So for us, the question became: how well can an LLM work with translations that cannot just be translated word for word back into the original? With such a base of books at our disposal, we used literature from the Gutenberg Project, where we found works from England, Germany, France, Italy, and the Netherlands. Project Gutenberg is a large online library consisting of books, which are not under any copyright, mostly due to the age of the text. It is reasonable to assume that the most current LLMs were trained on this library as it is easy to access and has most of the well-known classics in its database. 

For the project we took the model through different types of tests, assessing its capabilites. The focus was to test reproducability of training data, but with some obstacles, making it more difficult. The main approach was to test the model on text parts and asking for a decision on a multiple-choice basis. A model that encountered the book during training should do this well, while a model that never saw the book can still guess from the names and places in the passage. Random guessing would yield 25\%.

We ran this test with Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct on 100 books each in English,
German, French, Italian and Dutch. On top of these five runs we carried out
three smaller experiments. In the first, we took 23 German translations of
English books and asked the model for the English original title. In the
second, we replaced characters in the passages with random letters
and observed the effect on accuracy. In the third, we required the model to
explain its answer on smaller sets of books in English, German, and French.

\section{Related Work}
Chang et al. investigated which books ChatGPT and GPT-4 have seen between books in and out of the copyright\cite{chang2023}. They took short passages from novels, masked a single character name and let the model fill in the missing name. When a model supplies the correct name repeatedly, it has most likely encountered the book during training. They found that the models have memorised a large number of books and that the books whose passages appear most often on the web are memorised best. Models were tested on copyrighted and free books, where especially the open books had good results. This paper implied a large background knowledge of LLMs regarding well-known literature, which was an important starting point for our testing, assuming that the model had seen the books in training. Our setup also used a similar experiment approach, but focusing on titles instead. We also extended the testing towards another model,  as most paper used ChatGPT as base model.
Zhang et al. studied the gap between English and other languages in large language models \cite{zhang2023}. They gave the same tasks to the models in several languages and compared the results.For some tasks the correct answer stays the same after translation, while for others it depends on the language itself. On these language-dependent tasks the  models had clear difficulty once the question was no longer in English. The paper comes to the conclusion, that english functions as the models native language. Eventhough reasoning and knowledge should supposedly be independent from languages, english had consistently higher accuracies. The paper also speculates whether the models internally translate inputs into english. This would be a problem for the models in our tests, as books are usually not translated word for word, therefore this approach to literature analysis would be more complicated for the models. Also, because titles in different languages are not necessarily just translations of each other, but at times, something completely different. 
But not all paper were critical towards the results of LLMs in different languages. Shi et al. had a more positive take on the language results, regarding multi-language reasoning\cite{shi2022}. They tested chain of thought reasoning in different languages, and hat really good results, even in languages that make up a very small percentage of the training corpus. In the end of their paper they also tested how well models could understand the sense of words across different languages, where they had better results, than previous research when applying chain of thought. But they still concluded that this is a difficult task. Something we later apply towards literature translations, as human translations usually translate the sentiment but not necessarily the words, of a text.
Despite language tests in models being done in large quantities, there still seems to be a high dependence of the results on the task given. While training a model on given good reasoning, seemed to give good results, we now wanted to test, when the model was only trained on the texts, but not the task it was supposed to perform. Based on prior research and our own tests with the model, we performed tasks similar to the related works, but implementing different kinds of obstacles for the model, instead of a straighforward approach. 

\section{Approach}
\subsection{Reasearch Question and Goals}
The primary objective we intended to investigate was: How accurate are LLMs at performing zero-shot literary identification when input and output are supposed to be different languages. While models show strong performance in one singular language, we wanted to extend this testing to cross-lingual. While working on this we developed an additional question.  How resilient are LLMs capabilites when tasked with identifying corrupted literary text? Old digitized archives, but also old grammar or regional dialects, can lead to errors or intentional differences in text, which possibly hinder the recognition process. Therefore we tried to classify how much corruption still yields acceptable results. 
To answer this questions we built an automated benchmarking pipeline, using data streamed from Project Gutenberg. The results are visualized via graphs for later analysis. To ensure reproducibility and interactive execution we wrote the program into a Jupyter Notebook, which not only made the graphics easier to display but also enables smaller checks, without running the whole program. 

\subsection{Model and Database}
For the database, we searched through Hugging Face for appropriate literature databases to use. First, we wanted to use nikolina-p/gutenberg\_clean\_en\_splits\footnote{https://huggingface.co/datasets/nikolina-p/gutenberg\_clean\_en\_splits}, because the dataset already contained only books in English and had stripped all book headers. At first, this seemed like a good approach to make sure the model would only be asked about actual text parts, and not accidentally some part of the header which did not contribute to the book itself. Unfortunately, the books in this database were only identifiable by the numbers anymore, as title and similar things were contained in the header. That is why we switched to manu/project\_gutenberg\footnote{https://huggingface.co/datasets/manu/project\_gutenberg/viewer/default/en}, which was the original database used by nikolina-p/gutenberg\_clean\_en\_splits. This dataset holds the raw text of Project Gutenberg books, with additional information in the header text. The dataset was streamed, so that the code reads one book at a time over the network.
The model is Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct \cite{qwen2025}, an open instruction tuned model with 7 billion parameters that we run on a local machine through the transformers library. It was especially chosen for its known good performance, and its size of parameters. This size of model is still reasonable to execute on a local computer. 

\section{Experiment}
\subsection{Data Preprocessing}
Our program starts by implementing the key methods later used to test accuracy. These methods aim to be highly reusable, so as little work as possible will later be necessary during the actual experimentation. For this we state some variables in the beginning, which will be relevant for the rest of the experiment. These are especially taking 100 books per language and cutting a 1000 character long text snippet from the book. Both variables can be changed for more detailed research, but we chose them for a reasonable performance of our program, as larger numbers could possibly lead to better results, but also exceed our computing capacity. For preprocessing the books, we developed the method \textit{load\_books}, which took the target language as input and automated the rest of the loading process. In this method, we worked especially with regular expressions to extract the text passages and relevant informations about the book. These were especially extracting titles, via the search for the keyword \textit{Title} and checking whether a book was a translation by checking for a Translator. 

\begin{minted}{python}
    translator_match = re.search(r"Translator:\s*(.*)", text, re.IGNORECASE)
\end{minted}

Additional to the title, a text snippet was extracted from the middle of the book. By starting from the half of the text, we ensured that no header was cut out. Having header information in the input prompt would have falsified the results. 
This strategy was used to create the whole base for question creation, except for the cross-lingual part. For this an additional method was created. The approach is similar to the base code, but instead of searching for books with no translator we specifically searched for german books that were translated from the english language. A script first searched the German split for signs of a translation from English. It matched phrases like \textit{aus dem Englischen} in the opening part of the file and also flagged books that carry a translator entry together with an author from a list of well known English writers. The script wrote every hit to a CSV file, after which we filled in the English original titles by hand. For some hits no clear original could be established, so the final list holds 23 usable books, the final table is compiled in the Appendix. The english titles were then loaded in, and used for the next step, of creating the questions for evaluation. 
For the last part of our analysis we also built a noise generating function. This takes a percentage of noise, that is supposed to be added to the text. The percentage gives how many of the characters of the text are replaced by random characters. The noisy text is than returned to be used as a text snippet.
\begin{minted}{python}
    text_chars[idx] = random.choice(random_pool)
\end{minted}

\subsection{Evaluation Method}
To test the accuracy of the model every book becomes a single multiple choice question that holds the passage and four titles, exactly one of which is correct. The three wrong titles are drawn at random from the other 99 books of the same language set. The four options are then shuffled and labelled A to D. Because the wrong options are real titles in the same language, the model can not come to  the answer by language alone. The prompt states that the task is to identify the book behind a passage, then presents the passage and the four options and asks for a single letter. We allow the model to generate at most 4 tokens with greedy decoding so that the answer stays short and stable. A regular expression extracts the first letter from A to D in the reply and when no letter is found the question counts as an error. Across the five base runs and the translation run, this case never came up, as every answer contained a readable letter. By testing with multiple choice answers we ensure test robustness, avoiding clean-up of the answer to avoid minor formatting differences. Additionally we do not have to use much tokens, and the text generation works much faster. 

\begin{promptbox}{Prompt Template}
\small
\vspace{0.5em}
\textbf{User Prompt:} \\
\texttt{You are a helpful literature assistant.}\\
\texttt{Your task is to identify to which book a text snippet belongs to.}\\
\texttt{Respond only (!) with the single letter choice (A, B, C or D), corresponding to the correct book.}\\
\texttt{Do not output anything else.}\\
\texttt{"""}\\
\texttt{\{text\_snippet\}}\\
\texttt{"""}\\
\texttt{Which book is this snippet from?}\\
\texttt{A) Title A}\\
\texttt{B) Title B}\\
\texttt{C) Title C}\\
\texttt{D) Title D}\\
\texttt{Answer (A/B/C/D):}
\end{promptbox}

In the base test the model prints a single letter and nothing else. We also wanted to observe how it reasons. In this variant the model must answer in a fixed format with the letter first and three or four sentences of explanation after it and the limit rises to 200 new tokens instead of 4.

\section{Analysis}
\subsection{Accuracy Results}

First we established our baseline result, seen in Figure \ref{fig:english_chart}. Our plain assumption was that the model recognizes
literature best in English. Therefore to judge the accuracy of the model in other languages we will have to compare it to the baseline accuracy of english literature. Using english literature (originally written in the english language) and testing it for title prediction we observed a rather high accuracy of 89\%. 
\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{images/Languages.png} 
    \caption{Distribution of predictions, for different language literature}
    \label{fig:language_chart}
\end{figure}

Comparing the different languages now shows a significant difference in the models accuracy to predict titles from german, french and italian literature. Figure \ref{fig:language_chart} illustrates the zero-shot evaluation results. While none of the results appear to be especially bad, there is a visible gap between them and the english accuracy. Dutch scored higher than we expected. As Italian and Dutch are the two smallest of the datasets we chose, italian having 1010 books included and dutch 1420, this may be an interesting find of smaller booksets maybe being better stored in the models and easier to reproduce. Another possible interpretation could be that the books from this languages are more famous ones. While in Project Gutenberg all kinds of books can be saved this means that for english literature also very niche texts are stored. For less represented languages this could instead leading to only being represented by their most famous literature. 

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{images/German_trans.png} 
    \caption{Distribution of predictions, for german text -> english titles}
    \label{fig:language_chart}
\end{figure}
Comparing accuracy of german text parts with their original english titles, shows an accuracy that is significantly higher than the corresponding german literature. This finding suggests that english literature is far more known in any language than other original literature. The model can link German text to an English title when the book is well known, which fits the finding of Chang et al. \cite{chang2023}. Famous books have many copies on the web in several languages so the model had many opportunities to learn the link during training. 

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{images/noise.png} 
    \caption{Introducing noise}
    \label{fig:noise-chart}
\end{figure}
The noise run presents an interesting find, seen in Figure \ref{fig:noise-chart}. For this evaluation the accuracy of each language was tested with noise levels being raised in 5\% steps (0\%, 5\%, 10\%, etc...). While it is not surprising, that english remained to have the highest accuracy rate, it is the only one that does not at any point falls to around 25\% where the results are equally as good as guessing. Instead the results suggest that english literatuer is acutally still recognized with a 50\% chance, when almost half of the characters were misplaced. All other languages follow a relatively similar decline in accuracy, with all of them falling below 50\% accuracy at 25\% text corruption. French is a bit earlier at this, but this can be attributed to the samples being random books which can lead to slight fluctuations in the results. Also interesting is that the accuracy in dutch had a steep decline, despite dutch being quite similar to english in the beginning it has a similar accuracy with the other languages when being corrupted. This supports our theory that for the smaller dataset the model is better capable of just reproducing exact text passages, which gives the model difficulties when the text is slightly altered.  
A reason for some of the results we got, can be found, when analyzing the reasoning of the model behind some title decisions. A minor note at this is that we found a larger error rate in the model when the model was asked to explain its decision. When analyzing reasoning of the models (answers found in the Appendix), we found that two points influenced the models decision at most. One of them were certain characters that the model mapped to certain books. An example is the following quote from the explanations: "The text snippet is in German and appears to be from a play about Medea, a character from Greek mythology. The dialogue includes references to Peronto, Aietes, and mentions of Delphi and Colchis, which are locations from Greek mythology. This content aligns with the study of Greek antiquity, making "griechische altertumskunde" (Greek antiquity) the most appropriate choice among the given options." Here the actual book would have been \textit{Der Gastfreund} a book that plays in the ancient Kochis and mentions the Argonauts, but because of these well known characters the model is confused which book this could be, as it assosciates these characters with a limited number of works, here specifically ancient greek tales. This lets us assume that the model is not capable of actually reproducing or remembering text passages it encountered during training. Instead it associates certain books with certain traits, like characters and biggest part we found in our analysis: style. When asked to explain itself, the model almost always argumented with the style of a book fitting to a certain book. This implies the model learns during training to associate different writing styles with certain authors or certain works. Certainly this means the model reducing its accuracy for less known books, which styles maybe get interpreted as a better well known book, with a similar style, as writing style is something that is hard to define directly. For example in the (wrong) reasoning for the book \textit{midi à quatorze heures histoire d'un voisin--voyage dans paris--une visite à l'arsenal--un homme et une femme} the model argues, that: "The passage discusses social class and fashion in 19th-century France, reflecting themes common in Dumas's works." Therefore it argues the book is surely by Alexander Dumas, which is wrong as it is by Alphonse Karr. While he is not an unknown author, he is less known as Alexander Dumas, which seems his writing to be wrongly attributed to Dumas. This forgetting of \textit{smaller} authors matters regarding fairness. As we already stated, this probably comes from a frequency bias, where well known authors are better represented in the trainings data. With merging different works under one larger, well known author, literature history is forgotten by these models. It can also lead to the reinforcement of biases, as smaller authors, will be forgotten, hiding more hidden history, under the general well-known one. It also should caution to using LLMs in literary history analysis, as this findings indicate that models are not good at analyzing style movements and similar, because historical writing styles were often influenced by place and time. The fact that the error rate increases, indicates that during the reasoning the model actually gets caught up itself in its generalizing assumptions. These results are a caution towards digital humanities, and using LLMs when discovering literary and cultural history. 


\section{Conclusion}
Some limitations in our model remain. Every run is a single pass so the numbers carry sampling luck and with 100 books a swing of a few points between repeated runs would be normal. The titles come from the file headers and were lowercased, so a few odd or broken titles may have entered the question sets. The three wrong titles per question are random draws, which makes some questions easy when the wrong titles fall far from the topic of the passage. Still the results show a trend that could be further explored. As this experiment was limited by time and computing capacity. With more computing capacity the experiment could be expanded to larger and more models. Additionally with a bit of work an own database could be established, compiling more non-european centric works. We were also limited due to our own spoken languages in testing the application of titles to translated work. With more people available one could test how well models perform on english classics translated into other languages, but the mapping of the titles has to be done by hand, as it involves research into old books, as titles are not always just translations of the original one. The testing for noise could also be more finetuned, by for example strategically replacing character names with others or changing the writing style. This would lead to the creation of a handcrafted database, which could give more interesting insights. 
Nevertheless our experiment yielded interesting results. We built a small test around one clear question. Does a language model recognize books in other languages as well as it does in english. 
The answer to that was no. English reached a high percentage while the other four languages landed between 60\% and 80\%. The model beats random guessing everywhere, so the ability does not vanish outside English but it does weaken. We could show a noticable gap in language performance. A gap that even got larger, when done on corrupted text, which makes LLMs not well equiped for helping in digitalizing old literature outside of the english language. The model links German translations
to their English originals when the books are famous, it breaks down when half
of the text is random noise and its explanations sound convincing even when
they are wrong. Interestingly, the model achieved its highest cross-lingual accuracy (95.7\%) when matching German translations to their original English titles, reinforcing prior findings on the strong web representation of famous English works. Finally, qualitative analysis of the model’s reasoning highlighted a reliance on stylistic writings and well-known author biases rather than actual textual memorization. These limitations emphasize the need for caution when deploying Large Language Models for digital humanities and non-English literary analysis.
